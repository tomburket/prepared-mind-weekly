AI · Supply Chain
Hershey Cut $100 Million in Inventory. The Tool Was a Decision-Intelligence AI That Had Never Been Used in CPG at This Scale.
Supply Chain Dive · Antone Gonsalves · April 16, 2026
At its 2026 Investor Day on March 31, Hershey's Chief Supply Chain Officer Jason Reiman told investors the company has deployed decision-intelligence software across its supply chain — and projects $100 million in inventory reduction and $50 million in productivity gains over the next two years. The system gathers and analyzes supply chain data and surfaces alerts for operations managers: when to restock packaging, when production is getting ahead of plan, when demand signals shift. Reiman described it as a shift from reporting what happened to recommending what to do next. The technology is embedded in daily operations across sourcing, manufacturing, delivery, and planning.
Why it matters
A $100 million inventory reduction target at a major CPG company is a board-level number. The technology is not experimental — it is in production. The question for operators is no longer whether this class of tool works but how fast they are moving toward it.

CEO Market
Kroger Took a $2.6 Billion Charge to Walk Back Its Biggest Automation Bet. Here's What It Learned.
Grocery Dive · Peyton Bigora · November 18, 2025
In November 2025, Kroger announced it would close three of its Ocado-powered automated fulfillment centers — in Wisconsin, Maryland, and Florida — and take a $2.6 billion impairment charge. The goal: improve e-commerce profitability by $400 million in 2026 by shifting toward in-store fulfillment and third-party delivery partnerships with Instacart, DoorDash, and Uber. The Ocado partnership, launched in 2018, was built on the premise that centralized, highly automated customer fulfillment centers could make online grocery profitable at scale. After a comprehensive review, Kroger concluded the economics didn't hold at the volume and density it had. The pivot is toward capital-light, store-based automation in high-volume markets — essentially the opposite architectural bet.
Why it matters
The lesson isn't that automation is wrong. It's that automation sized for a demand curve that didn't materialize locks in costs that stores don't. The physics of grocery e-commerce still haven't been solved. Kroger just paid $2.6 billion to update its assumptions.

Kroger Just Launched 80+ Private Label Protein Products. The Refrigerated Aisle Led All Categories in Private Label Growth Last Year.
Grocery Dive · September 2025 & January 2026
Private label set a new unit volume record in 2025 — 68.7 billion units, per PLMA data cited by Grocery Dive in January. The refrigerated department led all categories in private label dollar sales growth at over 6%, followed by beverages at nearly 5%. PLMA President Peggy Davies: "Private label growth reflects a shift in consumer priorities, as retailer-owned brands increasingly compete — and win — on value, quality, health, and sustainability." Kroger made the most explicit strategic move: in September 2025, the company launched Simple Truth Protein — 80+ high-protein meals and snacks across frozen, beverages, chips, and desserts, described by Ann Reed, Group VP of Our Brands, as "the widest private label offering of protein products on the market." Kroger isn't waiting for national brands to lead the protein merchandising shift.
Why it matters
Protein has become a cross-category, cross-aisle organizing principle. The retailers who treat it as a standalone department rather than a planogram-wide lens are already behind.

GLP-1 Users Are Reshaping the Grocery Basket. Smaller Retailers Are Already Moving Fixtures.
Grocery Dive · January 2026 & Cornell University / Numerator / Acosta · April 2026
As of early 2026, 12.4% of U.S. consumers are on a GLP-1 weight loss drug — approximately 31 million current users, up from 5.8% in 2024, per Hartman Group research cited in FoodNavigator-USA. A Cornell University study analyzing transaction data from 150,000 households found that GLP-1 households reduce grocery spending by 5.3% within six months — with steepest declines in savory snacks and sweets — while outspending non-users by 25% on protein shakes and 9% on meat, beans, and yogurt. Conagra became the first major food company to add GLP-1-friendly labeling in January 2025, adding "On Track" badges to 26 Healthy Choice frozen meals. Anne Mezzenga, co-CEO of Omni Talk, told Grocery Dive that smaller retailers are already "changing their floor plans to highlight higher-protein items in a bid to cater to GLP-1 users" and moving refrigerated units housing protein drinks and yogurts into checkout areas.
Why it matters
The pharmacist is now influencing what goes in the cart. Grocery operators who wait for this to become obvious at the chain level will be redesigning fixtures two years behind the independents.

A Rock Drilled on Mars in 2020 Just Yielded the Most Diverse Collection of Organic Molecules Ever Found There.
NASA JPL / Nature Communications · April 21, 2026
After years of analysis, NASA's Curiosity team published results this week from a Martian rock sample drilled in 2020 at a site once covered by ancient lakes. Of 21 carbon-containing molecules identified, seven were detected for the first time on Mars — including a nitrogen heterocycle, a ring structure considered a chemical precursor to RNA and DNA. Scientists cannot determine whether the molecules were produced by biological or geological processes. Both remain possible. The findings were published Tuesday in Nature Communications.
Why it matters
They drilled the rock in 2020. The paper published in 2026. The answer is still: we don't know. That's not a failure — that's what rigorous science under genuine uncertainty actually looks like.

TED 2026 Recap: What becomes scarce?
Strange Loop (Adam Cunningham) · April 18, 2026
Adam Cunningham — Global CEO of Allied Global Marketing and writer of the Substack newsletter Strange Loop — published a 5,000-word post-conference essay on April 18 synthesizing talks by Esther Perel, Jonathan Haidt, Neil Katyal, Steve Huffman, Keke Palmer, and others into a single argument: "Systems are getting better at prediction, automation, simulation and scale. Humans are getting worse at ambiguity, presence, friction, patience and reciprocity." His reading of the "All of Us" theme is implicitly critical — TED 2026 diagnosed what technology is doing to human life with precision and stopped short of any mechanism for collective response. His closing: "The systems are getting better. The real question is whether we get better at being human while they do."
Why it matters
The gap between what systems optimize and what humans are actually for keeps widening. TED named it. Nobody answered it.

Cultural Signal
The Dams Came Down in 2024. By March 2026, the Salmon Were Already Hatching.
Klamath Tribes News · Herald and News · November 2024 – March 2026
In March 2026, the Klamath Tribes' Ambodat Department confirmed the first naturally hatched Chinook juveniles in Upper Klamath Lake tributaries in over a century, captured in rotary screw traps on the Sprague and Williamson rivers. The four dams that had blocked them came down in 2024. Adults were detected moving upstream within days of removal. Chairman William Ray Jr. asked tribal members to leave the c'iyaals undisturbed during spawning. The Tribes issued one statement about what the hatch means: "The c'iyaals' survival is not just an environmental story. It is a continuation of our own."
Why it matters
Juvenile salmon imprint the precise chemical signature of their natal water — a molecular map they carry for life. Remove the barrier, restore the water chemistry, and the navigation system executes. Scientists expected reestablishment to take generations. The fish were two years.

Oddly Interesting
A Medieval Football Game With No Rules, No Referee, and No Time Limit Just Completed Its 2026 Season in Workington, England
The Athletic · Rob Tanner · April 17, 2026
Uppies and Downies has been played in this coastal town in northwest England since the 17th century — through both world wars, every year until the pandemic. No rules, no boundaries, no time limit, unlimited players. The two-pound ball is stitched from cow leather and stuffed with wool flock. Victory requires throwing it in the air three times. This year's deciding game ran from daylight into darkness before Sam Williamson slipped out of the scrummage undetected, hid the ball in his trousers, ate a sweet to look casual, jumped into a stream, scratched through a thorny thicket, and ran to the goal. The mines and docks that once divided the two teams are long gone.
Why it matters
The mines closed. The docks closed. Easter still comes.
