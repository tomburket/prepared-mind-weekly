CEO Market
Kroger's New CEO Used His First Earnings Call to Define the Turnaround.
Grocery Dive · Sam Silverstein & Catherine Douglas Moran · March 10, 2026
In his first earnings call as CEO of the nation's largest supermarket chain, Greg Foran — who previously ran Walmart U.S. and Air New Zealand — told investors Kroger needs to focus on giving customers "a compelling reason to shop with you by offering great value, great products, and a great experience." He outlined five priorities: sharpen price perception, scale e-commerce toward profitability, expand private label, increase execution speed through a new Global Capability Center, and invest $3.8 to $4 billion in stores. His framing was operational and direct. "Customers need to trust that they're getting a fair deal every time they walk into our stores." Kroger's digital sales have grown more than 16% for five straight quarters, but the business hasn't turned a profit on e-commerce. Foran expects to fix that in the first half of 2026.
Why it matters
Boards at the largest food retailers are betting on operators, not strategists. Foran's appointment and his first-call language are a data point on what grocery boards currently want at the top.

The Grocery Industry Had a Volatile March at the Top. The Pattern Is Worth Watching.
Grocery Dive · March 31, 2026
Grocery Dive catalogued ten senior leadership changes in March alone. The headline move: JJ Fleeman, CEO of Ahold Delhaize USA — parent of Stop & Shop, Food Lion, and Giant — announced he will depart in June to become CEO of Dollar General in January 2027. Dollar General explicitly cited grocery as a growth priority in naming him. At Raley's, CEO Keith Knopf departed abruptly after seven years; owner and chairman Mike Teel stepped back in as chief executive. Heritage Grocers named David Hinojosa as CEO, filling a vacancy open since July 2025. Brookshire Grocery's Brad Brookshire retired after nearly 50 years with the company, replaced on an interim basis by the COO. Brookshire Brothers reorganized its entire C-suite in a single announcement.
Why it matters
When discounters recruit grocery CEOs and regional chains can't keep the seat filled, the market is telling you something about where the pressure is. The operators who know how to run lean, build culture, and hold margin in a difficult environment are in demand.

Consumer Products CEOs Lasted an Average of Six Years Before Exiting. 2025 Was the Worst Turnover Year in Recent Memory.
Spencer Stuart Consumer Products Leadership Index · March 2026
Spencer Stuart's Consumer Products Leadership Index — covering the top 50 global consumer products companies — found that average CEO tenure at time of exit is just six years, compared to nine years for large U.S. companies broadly. In 2025, that pressure became acute: Spencer Stuart's March 2026 report "15 New CEOs, One Turbulent Year" documented the highest CEO turnover in years for the sector, with one-third of external hires being proven CEOs recruited from other consumer products companies — a sign boards are reaching for turnaround experience over internal development. Sixteen of 24 Americas-based consumer products CEOs in the index were COO immediately before being named CEO. Marketing is the dominant functional background — 40% of those COOs came from marketing careers. Technology backgrounds are rising but still thin: technology leaders on the executive teams of the top 50 consumer products companies grew from 25 to 28 between 2023 and 2025.
Why it matters
When boards are pulling in proven outside CEOs at a record rate and tenures are running six years, the operators who have turned around complex organizations and kept cultures intact are exactly the profile in demand. The question every search committee is asking: who has actually done this before?

Beyond Meat Is Now Selling Protein Drinks. Impossible Is Thinking About Adding Beef. The Category Reached a Crossroads.
Food Dive · February 2026 & Wall Street Journal · June 2025
In January 2026, Beyond Meat launched Beyond Immerse — sparkling protein beverages with 10 to 20 grams of plant protein per can — and explicitly repositioned as "Beyond The Plant Protein Company." By February, Food Dive reported the beverage line had expanded after a "promising test launch," citing protein drinks as a category that grew 122% between 2020 and 2024. Impossible Foods CEO Peter McGuinness went further in June 2025, telling the Wall Street Journal the company might make "a hybrid burger that's 50% beef" to capture flexitarian consumers — and later told audiences the alt-meat market "was mismarketed and mislaunched." In January 2026, Impossible announced a licensing deal with EQUII, a fermentation startup, to produce high-protein bread and pasta.
Why it matters
The demand signal didn't disappear when plant-based meat peaked. It shifted form. What consumers wanted was protein — and the companies that bet on meat-mimicry are now chasing them sideways into drinks, blends, and grain staples.

03Board & Governance
Board · AI
83% of S&P 500 Companies Now Disclose AI as a Material Risk. Only 3% of Their Directors Disclose AI Expertise.
The Conference Board & EY Center for Board Matters · April 2026
The Conference Board found that S&P 500 companies disclosing AI as a material risk jumped from 12% in 2023 to 83% in 2025. EY's Center for Board Matters found that Fortune 100 companies assigning AI oversight to a board committee nearly quadrupled in a single year — from 11% in 2024 to 40% in 2025. Yet only 36% of boards have implemented a formal AI governance framework, only 6% have established a dedicated AI committee, and just 2.7% of S&P 500 directors disclose AI expertise. Glass Lewis now requires companies to disclose how boards oversee AI and will review governance practices when insufficient oversight results in material harm to shareholders.
Why it matters
The board that names AI a risk without building the governance infrastructure to manage it has created a liability, not a disclosure. The CEO who walks in knowing what a formal AI governance framework actually requires — and which directors can assess it — is operating at a different altitude.

The Last Time Everyone Watched the Same Thing Was 2014.
Hollywood Reporter · Rick Porter · April 18, 2026
At the 2014 Oscars, Bradley Cooper's selfie with a group of A-listers became the most retweeted post in Twitter's history. The broadcast drew 43.74 million viewers — the Academy Awards' largest audience in 14 years. Hollywood Reporter's Rick Porter argues that night was the last peak of a shared popular culture. The data supports him. The 2026 Oscars drew 18 million viewers. The Grammys drew 14.4 million. Where 24 network and cable shows averaged 12 million or more viewers in 2013-14, today there are three. The culprit isn't quality — it's choice. Netflix alone released more than 60 English-language scripted series in 2019. Add algorithmic personalization and the result is a media landscape where no two households are necessarily watching anything in common.
Why it matters
Every brand built in the monoculture era assumed a shared cultural language. That language is gone. The consumer your loyalty program is trying to reach has a completely different media diet than the one sitting two rows over. Brand strategy that doesn't start from fragmentation isn't starting from reality.

Corporate America Developed an AI Tell. Researchers Found It in 73 Documents in a Single Quarter.
The State of Brand · Melissa Rosenthal · April 2026
Market intelligence platform AlphaSense analyzed corporate earnings calls, shareholder letters, and SEC filings and found a construction that had appeared rarely across two decades suddenly exploding in Q4 2025: "It's not just X, it's Y." Progressive called its purpose "not just a statement, it's a guiding principle." Royal Caribbean called disruptive technology "not just a tool, it's a capability." Seventy-three documents used the exact construction in Q4 2025 alone. One corporate communications advisor told Barron's it was "one of the biggest tells in AI." Melissa Rosenthal of content platform Outlever calls it the Great Flattening — language models trained on the statistical average of all corporate writing producing the statistical average of all corporate writing.
Why it matters
When every company sounds like the average of every other company, sounding like yourself becomes the rarest competitive advantage. The brands that opt out own the next decade.

Why Innovation-Driven Growth Isn't Guaranteed — and What the 2025 Nobel in Economics Says We Must Protect.
Nobel Prize.org · October 2025; Explained via YouTube
Joel Mokyr, Philippe Aghion, and Peter Howitt shared the 2025 Nobel Prize in Economics for explaining why sustained economic growth happened at all — and why it isn't the historical norm. For most of human history, innovations existed without generating lasting growth. Mokyr's answer: sustained growth requires two types of knowledge in active feedback. Propositional knowledge — understanding why something works. Prescriptive knowledge — knowing how to build it. When these two types operate in separate worlds, innovations go nowhere. Britain's Industrial Revolution happened when they finally converged. The Nobel committee warned that stagnation, not growth, is the historical default — and that sustained growth can be threatened by market concentration, restrictions on academic freedom, and resistance from groups whose interests are challenged by new technology.
Why it matters
Most AI implementations fail for the same reason pre-industrial innovations never compounded: the people who understand the systems don't know what the work actually feels like, and the people doing the work have no visibility into why the systems behave as they do. No feedback loop. No useful knowledge. Growth isn't the default — it has to be protected.

Gen Z Interns Are Doing the Math on AI in Real Time. Here's What They Say They're There to Learn.
KPMG 2026 Winter Intern Pulse Survey · Derek Thomas · March 25, 2026
KPMG's 2026 Winter Intern Pulse Survey — 361 interns, released March 25 — found that Gen Z interns ranked hands-on experience, mentoring, and network-building as the most valuable internship outcomes, and said those experiences cannot be digitized. When asked which skills they're focused on demonstrating alongside AI agents: critical thinking first, adaptability second, creative and strategic thinking third. Industry-specific knowledge ranked last. The market context is real: the Federal Reserve Bank of New York puts recent graduate unemployment at 5.7% in Q4 2025, with underemployment at 42.5% — a 5-year high. IBM is moving the other direction, tripling U.S. entry-level hiring in 2026. CHRO Nickle LaMoreaux's argument: not investing in early talent now means a hollowed-out pipeline later. KPMG's Derek Thomas: "Gen Z isn't in denial about what AI means for their careers — they're doing the math in real time."
Why it matters
The organizations still hiring interns aren't being charitable. They're recognizing that the skills interns are explicitly trying to develop — judgment, adaptability, relational intelligence — are exactly what organizations need more of as AI absorbs the rest.

Oddly Interesting
"Foodie" Is Dead. Here's What Killed It.
Eater · Jaya Saxena · September 24, 2025
Jaya Saxena's essay in Eater traces the arc of "foodie" from its emergence in the early 1980s — coined by Gael Greene and Paul Levy as a slightly self-mocking label for people who cared too much about food — to its current status as a term nobody wants. The word once carried genuine cultural weight: an identity, a community, a set of shared references. It died the way most cultural markers die, not with a single blow but through overuse, brand capture, and the algorithmic flattening of taste into content. Saxena's argument: "foodie" didn't just get tired. It got replaced by something more specific and more honest — people who care about where their food comes from, how it was made, and who made it, without needing a label to prove it.
Why it matters
When a cultural category collapses, the consumers it described don't disappear. They redistribute. The question for grocery operators is where the people who used to call themselves foodies ended up — and whether your assortment strategy can find them.
