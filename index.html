Stanford Studied 51 Enterprise AI Deployments. The Hard Part Was Never the Model.
Stanford Digital Economy Lab · April 2026
Researchers at Stanford's Digital Economy Lab interviewed executives and project leaders across 51 enterprise AI deployments in 41 organizations — cases that had moved beyond pilot stage and delivered measurable business value. Their finding: 77% of the hardest challenges were invisible costs — change management, data quality, and process redesign. Technology was consistently described as the easiest part. In 61% of cases, a prior failed AI project preceded the successful one, and those failures almost always stemmed from teams treating AI as a technology project rather than a process and change management project. A regional supermarket chain that deployed agentic AI to replace its human procurement function entirely saw waste fall 40%, stockouts drop 80%, and EBITDA margin double — not because of the model, but because the use case had clear success criteria and recoverable errors.
Why it matters
The organizations pulling ahead aren't running better models. They're redesigning workflows, clearing organizational blockers weekly, and tying adoption to corporate OKRs. The durable advantage is in the orchestration layer — the process and the people — not the algorithm.

02 Board & Governance
Governance
61% of Consumer Markets Directors Say at Least One Board Member Should Be Replaced. AI Readiness Is the Reason.
PwC · February 26, 2026
PwC's 2025 Annual Corporate Directors Survey, covering 67 directors on public consumer markets company boards, found that 61% believe at least one director on their board should be replaced — raising the question of whether current members are equipped to lead in the AI era. Nearly every major consumer markets company is deploying AI, PwC notes, but fewer can articulate how their tools are governed, how they're trained and validated, or who is accountable when systems go off course. Boards are being told they can no longer delegate AI oversight to the CIO or CMO. Supply chain oversight remains a board priority — 52% of directors say they discuss it at every meeting — but only 26% have conducted tabletop exercises to prepare for supply chain disruptions.
Why it matters
The governance gap isn't about technology fluency. It's about whether a board can ask the right questions when management brings an AI deployment for approval — and hold the answer to account. Sixty-one percent of directors think their own boards aren't there yet.

The Army Just Approved a New Combat Ration Built on Food Processing Technology That Doesn't Exist in Your Grocery Store Yet
DEVCOM Soldier Center / Soldier Systems · July 2025
In July 2025, the Army's DEVCOM Soldier Center transitioned its new Close Combat Assault Ration to DoD-wide procurement — a compact, high-density individual field ration built on two emerging processing technologies. Vacuum microwave drying removes water uniformly while preserving moisture and nutrient density, producing smaller items that stay palatable under field conditions. Sonic agglomeration uses vibration and compression to bond food molecules without binders, shrinking item size by 30 to 50 percent while maintaining ingredients and nutrition. Both were developed with civilian ration manufacturers and suppliers, funded by the Army to support manufacturing adoption. DEVCOM notes it will continue working with industry to expand menu varieties using these platforms.
Why it matters
The cheese powder in your snack food came from a military lab. So did the retort pouch, the energy bar, and the extended-shelf-life bread. Vacuum microwave drying and sonic agglomeration are the current leading edge of that pipeline. They will reach grocery shelves. The question is which companies get there first.

A NASA Engineer Turned YouTube Creator Just Launched a Free Science Curriculum for Every Public School Teacher in America
Good Good Good · April 13, 2026
Mark Rober used his TED 2026 talk in Vancouver to announce Class CrunchLabs, a free online STEM curriculum for grades 3 through 8, built around what he calls "hiding the vegetables" — engineering genuine curiosity before delivering the lesson. Rober, a former NASA engineer with a YouTube channel built on elaborate science demonstrations, said he has posted a free science or engineering video once a month for 15 years. The curriculum, backed by roughly $60 million in investment including a $7.2 million anonymous gift, is available at no cost to public school teachers through the CrunchLabs Foundation.
Why it matters
The distribution problem in education has always been getting the compelling thing in front of the kid who needs it. Rober's bet is that the YouTube attention model translates to the classroom if you give teachers the vehicle.

Cultural Signal
The Dams Came Down in 2024. By March 2026, the Salmon Were Already Hatching.
Klamath Tribes News · Herald and News · November 2024 – March 2026
In March 2026, the Klamath Tribes' Ambodat Department — whose name means "in, at, or near the water" — confirmed the first naturally hatched Chinook juveniles in Upper Klamath Lake tributaries in over a century, captured in rotary screw traps on the Sprague and Williamson rivers. The four dams that had blocked them came down in 2024. Adults were detected moving upstream within days of removal. Chairman William Ray Jr. asked tribal members to leave the c'iyaals undisturbed during spawning. The Tribes issued one statement about what the hatch means: "The c'iyaals' survival is not just an environmental story. It is a continuation of our own."
Why it matters
Juvenile salmon imprint the precise chemical signature of their natal water — a molecular map they carry for life. Remove the barrier, restore the water chemistry, and the navigation system executes. Scientists expected reestablishment to take generations. The fish were two years.

Cultural Signal
"Foodie" Is Dead. The Culture It Created Is Now Just Called Tuesday.
Eater (Jaya Saxena) · September 24, 2025
Jaya Saxena traces the full arc: from Craig Claiborne making restaurant reviewing into arts criticism, to Food Network launching in 1993, to Top Chef giving a generation a shared culinary vocabulary, to Instagram flattening a $3 taco and a plate of duck at Momofuku Ko to the same size on a grid. The word "foodie," she argues, died not from failure but from total victory — absorbed so completely into American culture that naming it became redundant. "Now when people say they're a foodie, I'm like, yeah, you, me, and my uncle," says Elazar Sontag, restaurant editor at Bon Appétit. What remains is the underlying premise: that food is worthy of active, curious engagement, regardless of what you call the people who practice it.
Why it matters
The consumer who learned to care where their food came from, who made it, and what made it special is the same consumer who reshaped the grocery store. That shift didn't reverse. It just stopped needing a name.

The Dodgers Stopped Printing Paper Tickets. A 1986 Expos Stub Is Still in Someone's Wallet.
The Athletic (Rebecca Tauber) · April 13, 2026
Earlier this month, the Los Angeles Dodgers ended paper tickets for season ticket holders, drawing attention to a shift already well underway across professional sports. The Athletic's Rebecca Tauber traces the change through the people who felt it most — including Josée Tellier, who has carried her ticket from a May 1986 Montreal Expos game in her wallet for forty years, tucked next to a photo of her mother. A 1947 ticket stub from Jackie Robinson's debut sold in 2022 for $480,000. A paper ticket from Michael Jordan's first NBA game fetched $468,000 the same year. Meanwhile, fans who asked stadiums for a printed version have sometimes been handed an 8x11 sheet of paper with a QR code on it.
Why it matters
Digital optimized for convenience. It didn't account for what a ticket stub actually is — proof you felt something worth keeping.

A College Town in Minnesota Gave Its Viral Recipe to the World. Not You, Starbucks.
The Guardian (Rachel Leingang) · April 13, 2026
Little Joy Coffee in Northfield, Minnesota — population 20,000, town slogan "cows, colleges, community" — created a raspberry danish latte for spring, posted a video showing exactly how it was made and what it cost, then invited any independent coffee shop on earth to steal the recipe. Hundreds did. The shop's map of participating locations now shows pins on every continent except Antarctica and has logged nearly 2 million views. The latte — raspberry syrup, double espresso, vanilla cream cheese foam, two raspberries on a skewer — costs $8 at the shop and about $2.46 to make at home. Owner Cody Larson's original concern was that no one would actually put it on their menu and the shop would "look like losers." The drink is now their top seller, beating a plain latte.
Why it matters
The "DIY or buy" series that launched the drink was built on a straightforward premise: show people what an $8 coffee actually costs to produce. The transparency didn't hurt sales — it generated them. Small independent operators sharing recipes and margin math with each other while the big chains watch from the outside is a different kind of competitive strategy. It worked in Northfield.
