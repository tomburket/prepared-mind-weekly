Walmart Is Installing Digital Price Tags Nationwide. Lawmakers in 24 States Want to Ban Them.
Grocery Dive / Inside Global Tech · March 2026
Walmart confirmed plans to bring electronic shelf labels to all 4,700+ U.S. stores by end of 2026. The rollout is running into a political headwind. The UFCW launched a national campaign in February to ban what it calls surveillance pricing, and lawmakers have introduced more than 40 bills across 24 states this year, already outpacing all of 2025. A federal bill would ban ESLs outright in large stores. A peer-reviewed study across 114 stores found surge pricing was basically nonexistent both before and after ESL adoption. Separate research shows ESL-enabled markdowns on perishables can reduce food waste by up to 21%.
Why it matters
The gap between the research and the headline is wide. The technology works and the waste case is real, but the political and labor narrative is moving faster than the evidence can travel. Any grocery operator deploying ESLs without a clear public story about what the technology does and does not do is ceding the framing.

AI Ops
Only 1 in 5 Supply Chain Leaders Reports Real Value From AI. The Differentiator Is Not the Technology.
BCG · February 2026
BCG surveyed 181 global supply chain leaders. Over 70% are investing in advanced planning systems. Only about one in five reports meaningful value. Just 7% report value from agentic or generative AI. The companies seeing 50%+ productivity gains all did the same thing: cleaned their data, redesigned workflows from scratch, and trained planners to work alongside the tools before deploying. Companies that skipped those steps saw negative returns.
Why it matters
AI accelerates whatever it is built on top of. Clean processes get faster. Broken processes get more expensively broken.

The First Real Agentic Use Case in Supply Chain: Autonomous Replenishment
Inbound Logistics · January 2026
Inbound Logistics polled dozens of supply chain executives on where AI will matter most in 2026. The consensus: autonomous end-to-end replenishment. Kearney's Americas practice leader called it the most transformative use case on the horizon. Walmart's senior director of last-mile delivery rated AI's operational impact at 7.5 out of 10. DHL's analytics VP gave it a 10, conditional on organizational readiness. The most telling line: success hinges on handling partner data chaos, not on the algorithms.
Why it matters
Replenishment is where AI meets the physical world: trucks, shelves, perishables, weather. Every executive who rated AI highest added the same qualifier: the score depends on whether the organization is ready, not whether the technology is.

3,800 Workers Just Shut Down One of America's Largest Beef Plants. First Meatpacking Strike in 40 Years.
AP / Reuters / Fortune · March 16, 2026
Workers at the JBS Swift Beef facility in Greeley, Colorado walked off the line, the first strike at a U.S. beef slaughterhouse since the Hormel strike of 1985-86. UFCW Local 7 members voted 99% in favor after eight months of failed contract talks. The plant handles up to 6,000 cattle per day, roughly 7% of JBS total U.S. beef capacity. Workers want wages that track inflation, affordable healthcare, and an end to out-of-pocket charges for required safety gear costing over $1,100 annually. The strike lands as beef prices hit record highs, the national cattle herd sits at a 75-year low, and processors report losses exceeding $300 per head.
Why it matters
The workers who do the most physically demanding work in the food system are asking for wages that track inflation and gear they do not pay for themselves. The company losing $300 per animal has limited incentive to settle. That gap does not surface in a spreadsheet. It surfaces when 99% of a plant votes to walk.

Walmart Is Training 1.6 Million Workers on AI. Not Replacing Them.
Fortune / Inc. · February 2026
Walmart announced free AI training for its entire 1.6 million U.S. and Canadian workforce through Google's AI Professional Certification program. The company committed nearly $1 billion to skills training through 2026. Chief people officer Donna Morris said it is unfortunate when companies use AI to replace workers instead of training them. Incoming CEO John Furner described the approach as people-led, tech-powered. The announcement landed the same week Amazon, Nike, and Home Depot collectively eliminated 17,575 jobs.
Why it matters
One company is training forklift operators and cart pushers in AI fundamentals. Others are cutting headcount the same week. The contrast is doing its own positioning work.

Investors Managing $55 Trillion Just Told Boards What They Expect on AI. Most Boards Are Not Ready.
EY Center for Board Matters · February 2026
Between October and December 2025, EY interviewed stewardship leaders from institutional investors representing $55 trillion in assets under management. Investors want to understand how boards govern AI, how oversight is structured, how directors are building fluency, and who from management briefs the board. Only 12% of Fortune 100 companies disclosed any board-level AI education in their 2025 proxy statements. Over half of investors raised concerns about AI eroding the leadership bench by replacing entry-level work.
Why it matters
$55 trillion in capital is telling companies to get specific or get questioned. The prepared version of a board conversation about AI looks very different from the improvised one.

NIL Killed the Cinderella. The Bracket Knows It.
ESPN / USA Today · March 2026
The 2026 NCAA Men's Tournament produced only four double-digit seed wins in the first round, the lowest since 2007. No team seeded 13th through 16th advanced past the opening round for the second straight year. On one Friday, all 16 favorites won, the first time in over two decades. Since NIL compensation began in 2021-22, small-conference teams beating power-conference opponents in the regular season has dropped 58.9%. The upset factory that defined March Madness has been structurally dismantled in four years.
Why it matters
When resources flow freely to incumbents, systems designed around competitive balance stop producing disruption. The surprise was not that the upsets disappeared. It is how fast they did once the rules changed.

The Lost Art of Talking to Strangers
The Guardian · February 2026
A long-form piece on the near-total disappearance of casual conversation between strangers in public spaces. Research shows that people who talk to strangers report higher well-being, stronger belonging, and more accurate understanding of how others think, yet most people actively avoid it, predicting incorrectly that the interaction will be awkward or unwelcome. The avoidance is self-reinforcing: the less we practice, the worse we assume we would be at it.
Why it matters
Making a stranger feel heard in 90 seconds is a skill that no algorithm replicates and most professionals have systematically optimized away.

How Far Back in Time Could You Understand English?
The Dead Language Society · 2026
A Substack newsletter built on a single conceit: the same blog post, rewritten as it would have appeared every hundred years going back through English history. Modern English gives way to Georgian, then Elizabethan, then Middle English, and at some point you realize you can no longer understand your own language. The drift is gradual and then sudden.
Why it matters
Language is never fixed. Comprehension has a shelf life. Every generation that insisted people communicate the right way was eventually proven wrong by the clock.
