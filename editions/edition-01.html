AI Becomes the Store Manager's Quiet Co-Pilot
Grocery Dive · Feb. 25, 2026
Grocers are shifting AI from customer-facing experiments into back-end workflows — labor planning, inventory, task management at store level. Kroger's "Sage" assistant consolidates schedules, time-off, paystubs, and performance insights into a single interface for frontline associates and managers.

AI · Labor
India Built the World's Back Office. AI Is Starting to Shrink It.
The New York Times · Feb. 27, 2026
AI is automating the white-collar work that made India's $300 billion outsourcing industry — six million jobs — the envy of the developing world. Tata Consultancy Services has shed more than 20,000 workers from its 2022 peak. One startup replaced 65 human recruiters with AI voice agents; headcount is heading to 25 even as revenue grows. University placement rates are falling. The country is racing to adapt before the displacement outruns the upskilling.
Why it matters
The clearest large-scale evidence yet that AI displacement of knowledge work is real, not projected. Physical operations don't offshore and don't automate cleanly. The operator's advantage gets stronger, not weaker.

Small Rituals Are What Hold Frontline Teams Together Under Pressure
Moonstar Workforce Advisory · Dec. 16, 2025
High-performing retailers are pairing AI-driven tools with deliberate cultural practices — daily huddles, micro-celebrations, peer support routines — as frontline resilience mechanisms during technology change and demand spikes. The finding: these rituals matter as much as pay and scheduling in keeping teams engaged.
Why it matters
Ritual design is a leadership competency, not a soft perk. When the operating environment is continuously reshaped by data and automation, the CEO who builds cultural shock absorbers wins on retention and execution.

Kansas City Just Became the Base Camp Capital of the World Cup. Nobody Saw It Coming.
KCUR · March 5, 2026
Kansas City — the smallest of the 16 World Cup host cities — has landed four national team base camps: Argentina, England, Netherlands, and Algeria. The region outcompeted cities with larger populations, bigger media markets, and more event experience. The answer is 15 years and $650 million in soccer infrastructure, combined with aggressive, unified civic leadership that opened a fourth base camp site to meet demand.
Why it matters
Long-term infrastructure investment plus disciplined execution beats size. A smaller market that wants it more, prepares longer, and shows up unified will outcompete a bigger one that assumes the job is already theirs.
